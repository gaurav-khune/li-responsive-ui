Early in my career, I measured progress by how much code I could write in a day. More features, more commits, more lines—it felt productive. Years later, I’ve learned that real technical growth looks very different. Today, progress often means writing less code, but thinking more. It’s about understanding trade-offs, anticipating failure points, and designing systems that are easy to maintain—not just easy to build. Some of the most impactful improvements I’ve made didn’t involve new frameworks or tools. They came from refactoring confusing logic, improving error handling, and having tough conversations about technical debt. In the IT industry, especially at scale, clarity beats complexity every time. If you’re building software for the long run:

  • Prioritize readability over cleverness
  • Invest time in fundamentals
  • Don’t rush solutions—understand the problem first

Good software is not just about making things work. It’s about making them last. Curious to hear—what changed the way you think about writing code?
